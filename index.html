01 — Data Foundations

Every AI system is an information retrieval system at its core.

What makes retrieval work is not only the embedding model, the chunking strategy, or the LLM. It's whether users trust the data being retrieved. Trust determines adoption. Adoption determines value.

This is the foundational principle behind every project in this portfolio — and the reason data foundations are treated as a first-class engineering concern, not a prerequisite someone else handles.

01.1

Identity Resolution & Master Data Management

Identity resolution is a graph problem. Each entity — a customer, a product, a supplier — is a node. Their transactions, identifiers, and attributes are connected via edges, each carrying metadata as attributes on the relationship. The matching rules determine which edges are drawn, and with what confidence.

At the UK's leading retailer, grocer, and Financial Services firm, this work meant designing deterministic and probabilistic matching rules for the Single Customer View program — resolving millions of customer transactions across channels into unified profiles. The harder problem was governance: translating business risk appetite into precise technical matching logic under GDPR. Looser rules capture more customers but introduce incorrect attribution risk. The outcome was a documented risk decision: the existing design was maintained, having established that additional marketing reach did not justify the compliance exposure.

01.2

Dependency Mapping

When a data product generates £595M in annual revenue, removing it from a legacy platform is not an engineering task — it is a risk management exercise.

This engagement at the UK's leading retailer meant mapping every dependency across 50+ data products spanning three legacy platforms, holding the full value chain in view before touching a single system, and sequencing changes to protect business-critical outputs throughout.

The skill at work is systems thinking: identifying which variables can be safely isolated, and removing them without collapsing the structure around them.

01.3

Data Value Measurement

Data teams default to measuring outputs: tables built, pipelines run, tickets closed. These metrics have no relationship to business value.

The value measurement framework developed at the UK's leading retailer applied product management principles to data: measure change in user behavior, not team activity. Four dimensions — availability, reliability, discoverability, and trust — with the last one being the hardest to engineer and the most consequential.

01.4

Data Modeling

The shape of a gold layer in a medallion architecture is not a technical decision — it is a business one. For a utilities company, this work involved designing a customer data model across bronze, silver, and gold layers in Starburst, with the gold layer structured as a star schema aligned to the business's analytical use cases. The distinction that drives every modeling decision: transactional databases are optimized for reading and writing data; dimensional models are optimized for analysis. Conflating the two is one of the most common reasons data products fail to deliver insight.

At the conceptual layer, the challenge is different: designing an entry point into a data catalog that matches the way users actually query for information — not how the data is physically stored. At a reinsurance firm, this meant building a conceptual model spanning 11 business units and 1,500 data concepts, with the catalog's knowledge graph representing physical data as conceptual metadata. Each concept is a node; relationships between concepts are edges; attributes describe what that concept means to the business. Unstructured knowledge has to be given a schema before it can be retrieved — defining what the nodes are, how they relate, which attributes are mandatory versus optional, and what the validation rules are for each field.

One of the more technically nuanced challenges in this engagement was managing the migration from pre-production to production environments within Collibra. The core problem: when the attributes of a node change during migration, the relationships that depend on those nodes risk being broken or silently lost. The analysis work here was designing the approach to ensure that relationships between nodes are persisted as attributes change — validating referential integrity across the graph before and after migration, and identifying which relationship types were most vulnerable to data loss during the transition.

02 — Solution Architecture

The work in this section represents end-to-end architecture and delivery of AI systems across regulated, data-complex industries. The consistent design principle: governance and auditability are architectural features, not afterthoughts.

Delivery Lead Global Pharma & Life Sciences

Clinical Trial Site Selection — 99.7% Reduction in Processing Time

A clinical operations team was spending up to three working days manually querying fragmented enterprise data to select trial sites. The data lived across disconnected systems, was inconsistently populated, and required manual reconciliation before any decision could be made.

Phase 1 solution: a Snowflake → Starburst data pipeline unifying fragmented datasets, a React conversational interface collecting parameters in natural language, an LLM-powered SQL generation layer translating those parameters into precise queries, and custom-built data substitution logic — where a preferred data value was missing, the system identified and applied the most defensible alternative based on defined rules. Audit trails documented the origin and lineage of every output.

Phase 2 architecture extended the system from a deterministic LangChain pipeline toward a LangGraph agentic framework — adding explicit state management, concurrent non-deterministic decision pathways, and built-in auditability. A system a clinical team cannot audit is a system they will not trust.

↳ Site selection time: 3 working days → 15 minutes (99.7% reduction)
Independently Originated Reinsurance

Metadata Intelligence — From Idea to Sold Product

Large organizations have a metadata problem: data catalogs are only useful if concepts are named, described, and consistently labeled. Generating that metadata manually requires extracting tacit knowledge from domain experts — slow, expensive, and inconsistently performed.

This tool was independently conceived, prototyped, and sold — with no existing brief. The architecture: RAG-style injection of trusted industry glossaries, multi-shot prompt examples, and deterministic rules into OpenAI API calls to control the accuracy and consistency of outputs. A human-in-the-loop evaluation framework allowed primary users to benchmark and iterate on prompt quality.

The business case was built from first principles: fully-loaded labor cost of manual metadata generation versus the investment in the tool. The client bought it.

↳ Manual metadata effort reduced by 70–80%
Commercial Lead Insurance

Nearest Neighbour Modelling — PoC Strategy & Commercial Proposal

Insurance underwriting and risk assessment depend on finding meaningful similarity between cases — a problem that is fundamentally a nearest neighbour problem. For a prospective insurance client, Izzy led the strategy and commercial proposal for a 6-week PoC, covering both the technical approach and the business case for investment.

The technical strategy addressed four interdependent decisions. First, modelling approach: how to define and measure similarity across a mix of structured attributes, and which distance metrics are appropriate for different data types. Second, attribute weighting: not all attributes contribute equally to meaningful similarity — defining the weighting logic requires domain knowledge about which features actually drive risk or outcome, not just statistical correlation. Third, normalization: before any distance calculation is meaningful, attributes must be normalized to a common scale — the approach varies significantly depending on whether the distribution is linear, skewed, or categorical. Fourth, data strategy: the decision between synthetic data and client production data for the PoC, with explicit trade-offs — synthetic data reduces risk and accelerates setup but may not reflect the edge cases that determine whether the model works in production; client data tests real-world performance but introduces data governance and privacy considerations from day one.

The commercial component required translating these technical choices into a client-facing investment case — framing the PoC scope, timeline, and success criteria in terms of business value, not model performance metrics.

Architecture Lead Kubrick — Internal

Internal Knowledge Management System

The question consultants ask most often: has anyone done something like this before? The answer lived in people's heads, Slack threads, and half-documented project folders.

The solution: a retrieval-based system allowing consultants to query past project experience conversationally. The architectural lesson was the same one that surfaces in every knowledge system — the retrieval layer is the easier problem. The hard problem is what comes before it: taking unstructured consulting experience and giving it enough structure to be retrievable at all.

That means asking the questions most teams skip. How do we define a schema for knowledge that has never had one? Which fields are mandatory — client industry, problem type, technology used — and which are optional? What are the data validation rules for each field, and who enforces them? How do we normalize inputs from consultants who describe the same kind of project in completely different terms? Governance of the inputs determines the quality of the outputs. This is true of every knowledge system ever built.

04 — Data Governance

Governance is an AI problem, not a compliance problem.

The organizations that extract the most value from AI are not the ones with the most sophisticated models. They are the ones whose users trust the data those models are built on. Ungoverned data creates systems that get abandoned. The failure mode is not regulatory — it's organizational.

At its core, data governance is a knowledge graph problem. Enterprise data — its concepts, relationships, ownership, lineage, and quality rules — is a network of interconnected entities. In Collibra, that structure is represented literally: data concepts as nodes, relationships as edges, metadata as attributes. Designing a governance framework means designing that graph: deciding what the nodes are, what relationships matter, what attributes are mandatory, and what rules govern how the network is traversed to answer a user's question.

This is the same design discipline as building a knowledge graph for AI. The domain changes. The structural logic is identical.

Governance work delivered across financial services, retail, and insurance: metadata standards, technical lineage, operating model design, and data ownership frameworks. At a reinsurance firm, automated lineage coverage was improved by 20–50% across four domains. The data catalog scaled to 11 business units, 1,500 data concepts, 12 Data Owners, 20 Data Stewards, and 45 Technical Stewards — with three personalized UX dashboards designed around persona-specific needs.

Operating model design means understanding that the right governance structure — centralized, federated, or decentralized — is a function of organizational risk profile and data domain maturity. Getting it wrong means policies that exist on paper and are ignored in practice.

The consistent lesson: build it and they will come is not a data strategy. Understanding what each persona is measured by, what they need to do their job, and what a good experience feels like to them — that is a data strategy.
